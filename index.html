Problem Structuring
MECE Principle
Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive — the bedrock logic for clean problem decomposition.
McKinsey Logic Structuring

What it is

MECE (mee-see) is a principle for organising information and ideas so that categories are Mutually Exclusive (no overlap) and Collectively Exhaustive (no gaps). Developed at McKinsey, it's the foundation of clean issue trees and structured thinking.

When to use it

  • Breaking down a client problem into workable parts
  • Structuring slide storylines and recommendations
  • Designing interview guides and data collection plans
  • Checking whether your hypotheses cover the full problem space

How to apply it

  1. State the core question clearly in one sentence
  2. Brainstorm all possible "buckets" that answer or explain it
  3. Check overlap — can any item belong to two buckets? If yes, restructure
  4. Check completeness — is there any possible answer not covered?
  5. Group remaining items and label each bucket at the same level of abstraction

Common pitfalls

  • Mixing levels (e.g. "Revenue" vs "Sales team performance" — not the same level)
  • Confusing MECE structure with a story — MECE organises, narrative persuades
  • Creating too many top-level buckets (3–5 is usually optimal)
  • Forcing MECE when the problem is genuinely messy — acknowledge this
Practice Mode

Apply the framework

Scenario: A retail bank is seeing declining profits. Your partner asks you to structure the problem. Using MECE, break this into its top-level buckets and explain why your categories are mutually exclusive and collectively exhaustive.

Problem Structuring
Issue Tree
A visual logic map that breaks a big question into smaller answerable sub-questions, driving to root cause.
Diagnosis Root Cause Visual

What it is

An issue tree is a hierarchical diagram that decomposes a problem statement into increasingly specific sub-questions. The top node is your key question; each branch breaks it into MECE sub-issues until you reach answerable, testable hypotheses.

Two types

  • Diagnostic tree: "Why is this happening?" — decomposes causes
  • Solution tree: "How do we fix this?" — decomposes options

Most real projects use both: diagnose first, then solution-tree the response.

How to build one

  1. Write the key question in a box on the far left
  2. Draw 3–5 branches: the major categories of explanation or solution
  3. For each branch, ask "what are the sub-components of this?" and branch again
  4. Continue until each leaf node is a testable hypothesis
  5. Prioritise branches: which are most likely to be the answer? Start there

Consulting application

  • Use at the start of every project to align the team on scope
  • Share with the client as a "here's how we're thinking about this" moment
  • Update as you learn — prune dead branches, deepen live ones
Practice Mode

Apply the framework

Scenario: A hospital network asks you why patient satisfaction scores have dropped 18% in 12 months. Build the first two levels of a diagnostic issue tree. List your top-level branches (Level 1) and the sub-questions under at least two of them (Level 2).

Consulting Approach
Hypothesis-Driven Thinking
Start with an answer, prove it wrong. The consulting alternative to exhaustive research.
Efficiency Mindset McKinsey Way

What it is

Rather than conducting exhaustive research before forming a view, hypothesis-driven consulting starts with a best guess (the hypothesis) and then tests it efficiently. This keeps work focused, avoids boiling the ocean, and allows early communication of direction to clients.

The mindset shift

  • Academic: "Let me gather all the data, then form a conclusion"
  • Consulting: "Here's our best hypothesis. What's the fastest way to prove it wrong?"

You're not being intellectually lazy — you're being directionally efficient.

The cycle

  1. Form a clear hypothesis: "We believe the problem is X because Y"
  2. Identify the 2–3 facts that would prove it wrong (kill criteria)
  3. Go collect only those facts first
  4. If the hypothesis survives, deepen. If it dies, revise and repeat
  5. Document what you learned at each step

In a client context

  • Share your hypothesis with the client early — it builds confidence and surfaces their knowledge
  • Frame updates as "our hypothesis has evolved" — shows rigour, not flip-flopping
  • The final recommendation should trace back to a clear initial hypothesis that either held or was refined
Practice Mode

Apply the framework

Scenario: You're joining a project on Day 1. The client is a logistics company with rising operational costs. You have 30 minutes before meeting the CEO. Write one clear hypothesis about the root cause, and list the 2–3 pieces of evidence you'd want to look at first to test it.

Communication
The Pyramid Principle
Answer first, support second. Barbara Minto's framework for executive communication.
Writing Slides SCQA

What it is

The Pyramid Principle (Barbara Minto, McKinsey 1987) holds that communication should lead with the conclusion, then support it with grouped arguments, then support each argument with data. The opposite of academic writing — and the backbone of consulting slide decks.

SCQA structure

  • Situation: Neutral facts about the current state (agree on context)
  • Complication: What has changed or is at risk (why this matters now)
  • Question: The question the complication raises
  • Answer: Your recommendation — delivered before the supporting argument

Building the pyramid

  1. Write your governing thought (the single recommendation in one sentence)
  2. Identify 3 key arguments that support it (no more — executives stop listening after 3)
  3. Under each argument, list the evidence and data that proves it
  4. Check vertical logic: does each level fully explain the level above?
  5. Check horizontal logic: are the points at each level truly parallel and MECE?

Slide application

  • Every slide title should be the "so what" — the insight, not the topic
  • "Revenue by region" ✗ → "Asia-Pacific is the only region growing" ✓
  • The body of the slide provides the evidence for that claim
Practice Mode

Apply the framework

Scenario: You need to present to a CFO who has 5 minutes. Your finding: the company should exit its European market. Write the SCQA opening (4 sentences max) and list your 3 supporting arguments in pyramid structure.

Prioritisation
The 2×2 Matrix
Four quadrants, two axes — the simplest tool for prioritisation and strategic communication.
Visual BCG Priority

What it is

A 2×2 matrix plots items on two dimensions to create four quadrants, each suggesting a different strategic response. The BCG Growth-Share Matrix is the most famous, but the format applies across nearly every consulting question.

Classic versions

  • Impact vs Effort: Quick wins, big bets, fill-ins, thankless tasks
  • Urgency vs Importance: Eisenhower Matrix — do, schedule, delegate, eliminate
  • Market Share vs Growth: BCG — Stars, Cash Cows, Question Marks, Dogs
  • Feasibility vs Desirability: Innovation portfolio prioritisation

How to use it well

  1. Choose axes that are genuinely independent (no hidden correlation)
  2. Define what "high" means on each axis before you plot
  3. Name the quadrants with action language, not descriptions
  4. Let the matrix drive the conversation, not conclude it — outliers are interesting

Common mistakes

  • Choosing axes that aren't actually independent (they correlate and everything clusters)
  • Using it as a decoration rather than a decision tool
  • Forgetting to define thresholds — where exactly is the high/low line?
Practice Mode

Apply the framework

Scenario: A government department has 8 digital transformation initiatives. Resources allow them to progress only 3 this year. Choose your two axes, name all four quadrants with action language, and explain where you'd place at least 3 types of initiatives.

Stakeholder Analysis
Stakeholder Mapping
Who matters, how much, and what they want. The political map every consultant needs.
Power Influence Change

What it is

Stakeholder mapping identifies who has a stake in the project, their level of power and interest, and their current disposition. It determines how much engagement each stakeholder needs and what form that engagement should take.

The power-interest grid

  • High power, high interest: Manage closely — these are your key players
  • High power, low interest: Keep satisfied — they can block but won't engage
  • Low power, high interest: Keep informed — advocates in waiting
  • Low power, low interest: Monitor — minimal effort needed

Beyond the grid: Disposition

  1. Plot each stakeholder by power and interest
  2. Mark their current disposition: Champion / Neutral / Resistant
  3. For each resistant high-power stakeholder, develop a specific engagement plan
  4. Identify your coalition of champions and activate them early
  5. Revisit the map at each project milestone — positions shift

In public sector / social impact consulting

  • Add a third dimension: legitimacy (who has the moral authority to speak on this?)
  • Community voices often have low formal power but high legitimacy — treat them accordingly
  • Document who is absent from the room and why
Practice Mode

Apply the framework

Scenario: You're advising a city council on closing three underperforming community health centres and consolidating into one modern hub. Identify 5 stakeholders, plot them on the power-interest grid, note their disposition, and describe your engagement approach for the most critical one.

Industry Analysis
Porter's Five Forces
Is this industry structurally attractive? The framework that reveals where power actually lives in any market.
CompetitionMarketsHarvard

The five forces

  • Threat of new entrants: How easy is it for competitors to enter? (barriers: capital, regulation, brand, IP)
  • Bargaining power of suppliers: Can suppliers squeeze margins? (concentration, switching costs, substitutes)
  • Bargaining power of buyers: Can customers push prices down? (volume, alternatives, switching ease)
  • Threat of substitutes: Could a different solution make this industry irrelevant?
  • Competitive rivalry: How intense is competition among existing players?

The output: structural attractiveness

After assessing all five forces, rate each High/Medium/Low. Industries where most forces are weak (low threat) are structurally attractive — incumbents can sustain profitability. Industries where forces are strong are structurally brutal (airlines, airlines, airlines).

How to use it in consulting

  1. Run it at the industry level first — not the company level
  2. For each force, identify the 2–3 specific factors that drive it high or low
  3. Identify which force is most critical to the client's problem
  4. Use to frame the "why now" of a market entry, exit, or strategy pivot
  5. Pair with SWOT: Five Forces is external, SWOT adds internal perspective

Common misuses

  • Applying it to a company rather than an industry
  • Treating all five forces as equally important — they rarely are
  • Forgetting the sixth force often added: Complementors (players who increase the value of your product)
  • Using it as a static snapshot — forces shift over time
Practice Mode

Apply the framework

Scenario: A private equity firm is evaluating entry into the Australian residential aged care sector. Run a Five Forces analysis — rate each force (High/Medium/Low), explain the 2–3 key drivers, and give a one-sentence conclusion on structural attractiveness.

Organisational Design
McKinsey 7S Framework
Seven interdependent elements that must align for strategy to execute. The diagnostic for when change keeps failing.
AlignmentOrganisationMcKinsey

The seven elements

  • Strategy: The plan for competitive advantage
  • Structure: How the organisation is divided and coordinated
  • Systems: Processes, workflows, and IT that run daily operations
  • Shared Values: The core beliefs and culture (the central element — everything connects to this)
  • Style: Leadership and management approach
  • Staff: People capabilities and talent pipeline
  • Skills: Competencies the organisation actually has vs needs

Hard vs Soft S's

  • Hard (easier to change): Strategy, Structure, Systems
  • Soft (harder to change): Shared Values, Style, Staff, Skills
  • Most implementations fail because they change the Hard S's and ignore the Soft S's

Using it in consulting

  1. Map the current state of all 7 elements
  2. Map the future state required by the strategy
  3. Identify the gaps — where is the misalignment?
  4. Sequence interventions: Hard S's first, but budget for Soft S's
  5. Check: if you change element X, which others are affected?

When to reach for it

  • Post-merger integration — two organisations with different Shared Values will clash
  • Strategy execution failures — "we have the strategy, why isn't it working?"
  • Organisational redesign — check that new structure aligns with strategy
  • Culture change programs — it exposes what actually needs to shift
Practice Mode

Apply the framework

Scenario: A regional hospital network has a strategy to become a digital-first health system within 3 years. They've invested in new EHR technology (Systems) and restructured into integrated care teams (Structure). But 18 months in, adoption is low and staff are resistant. Use 7S to diagnose the misalignment — identify which elements are out of sync with the strategy.

Business Model Design
Business Model Canvas
Nine building blocks, one page. The universal language for describing how any organisation creates and captures value.
OsterwalderStartupsStrategy

The nine blocks

  • Customer Segments: Who are we creating value for?
  • Value Propositions: What problem do we solve? What need do we satisfy?
  • Channels: How do we reach and deliver to customers?
  • Customer Relationships: What relationship does each segment expect?
  • Revenue Streams: What are customers paying for, and how?
  • Key Resources: What assets does the model require?
  • Key Activities: What must we do well to deliver the value proposition?
  • Key Partnerships: What can't we do ourselves — and who can?
  • Cost Structure: What are the most important costs?

Left brain vs right brain

  • Right side (value): Customer Segments, Value Props, Channels, Relationships, Revenue — who we serve and how
  • Left side (efficiency): Key Resources, Activities, Partners, Costs — how we operate
  • Business model innovation often means changing the left side to serve the right side differently

In consulting practice

  1. Always start with Customer Segments — everything else follows from who you serve
  2. The Value Proposition is the pivot: does it match what the segment actually needs?
  3. Check fit between Revenue Streams and Cost Structure — is the model viable?
  4. Map current state, then future state — the gap is your consulting work

For health and social impact

  • Often the "customer" (funder/government) differs from the "beneficiary" (patient/community) — map both
  • Add a "Social Value Proposition" block to capture mission alongside commercial value
  • Revenue Streams in this context often include grants, SHS contracts, fee-for-service, philanthropy
Practice Mode

Apply the framework

Scenario: A GP clinic wants to pivot from episodic fee-for-service consultations to a subscription-based chronic disease management model. Complete a Business Model Canvas for the new model — focus especially on how the Value Proposition, Revenue Streams, and Key Activities change from the current model.

Growth Strategy
Three Horizons of Growth
Manage today, build tomorrow, create the future — simultaneously. McKinsey's framework for balancing exploitation and exploration.
McKinseyInnovationPortfolio

The three horizons

  • Horizon 1 — Defend & Extend: Current core business. Optimise, defend market share, improve margins. Payoff: now. (60–70% of resources)
  • Horizon 2 — Build & Grow: Emerging opportunities that extend the core. Scaling proven bets. Payoff: 2–5 years. (20–30% of resources)
  • Horizon 3 — Create & Imagine: Exploratory options and disruptions. High risk, high potential. Payoff: 5–10 years. (10% of resources)

The key insight

All three horizons must be managed simultaneously — not sequentially. Organisations that only manage H1 become obsolete. Organisations that only pursue H3 run out of money. The portfolio balance is the discipline.

Common failure modes

  1. H1 gravity: Every resource request gets pulled back to the core — H2 and H3 starve
  2. H3 theatre: H3 activities exist as innovation washing but receive no real resources
  3. Wrong metrics: Measuring H2/H3 on H1 profitability criteria kills exploration too early
  4. Culture clash: H1 and H3 need different cultures — separating them is often necessary

Application

  • Map the client's current portfolio across all three horizons
  • Ask: are H2 and H3 underfunded relative to stated strategy?
  • Design different KPIs and governance for each horizon
  • Use to structure innovation investment conversations with boards
Practice Mode

Apply the framework

Scenario: A pathology company generates 85% of revenue from routine blood tests (fast commoditising under automation). They're considering: (A) Improving turnaround time on existing tests, (B) Building a genomic screening service for oncologists, (C) Researching AI-powered diagnostics for rare diseases. Map each to a horizon, recommend a resource allocation split, and identify their biggest Horizon 1 risk.

Environmental Scanning
PESTLE Analysis
Political, Economic, Social, Technological, Legal, Environmental — a checklist to make sure you've diagnosed the whole context, not just the obvious slice.
ContextMacroChecklist

The six lenses

  • Political: Government stability, regulation, trade policy, election cycles
  • Economic: Growth, interest rates, inflation, exchange rates, labour market
  • Social: Demographics, cultural trends, consumer attitudes, workforce values
  • Technological: Innovation rate, automation, digital disruption, R&D activity
  • Legal: Employment law, health and safety, industry-specific regulation, IP law
  • Environmental: Climate policy, sustainability expectations, resource scarcity

What it's actually for

PESTLE is MECE applied to context rather than to a single problem. It exists to stop you diagnosing a client's situation too narrowly — most people naturally default to thinking about competitors and customers (which Porter's Five Forces covers) and forget the macro forces sitting above the industry entirely.

How to use it well

  1. Run through all six lenses even if some feel irrelevant — the discipline is in checking, not just listing what's obvious
  2. For each factor identified, ask: is this a current reality or an emerging trend? Time horizon matters
  3. Rate each factor's likely impact and likelihood — don't treat all six as equally weighted
  4. Carry the 2–3 most material factors forward into your actual structuring work (Issue Tree, SWOT, etc.) — PESTLE is an input, not an endpoint

Common pitfalls

  • Treating it as a box-ticking exercise that produces a long list nobody uses afterward
  • Confusing PESTLE (macro/external context) with SWOT (which mixes internal and external) — they answer different questions
  • Listing factors without assessing materiality — not every PESTLE factor matters for every client problem

Apply the framework

Problem Structuring
Root Cause Analysis (5 Whys & Fishbone)
The symptom is rarely the problem. Two simple techniques for pushing past it to what's actually driving the issue.
5 WhysIshikawaCausation

The 5 Whys technique

Start with the presenting problem and ask "why?" repeatedly — typically five times — with each answer becoming the input to the next question. The discipline is resisting the urge to stop at the first plausible-sounding answer, which is usually still a symptom, not a root cause.

The Fishbone (Ishikawa) diagram

A visual structure that branches the problem into standard cause categories — commonly People, Process, Equipment, Materials, Environment, Management — then asks "why" within each branch. Useful when a problem likely has multiple contributing causes rather than one linear chain.

How this relates to Issue Tree

  1. Issue Tree decomposes a problem into MECE categories of possible explanation, broad to narrow
  2. Root Cause Analysis goes deep on one branch, asking "why" repeatedly until you hit something actionable
  3. In practice: use Issue Tree to identify which branch is most likely the answer, then 5 Whys or Fishbone to drill into that specific branch
  4. Stop when the answer becomes something you can actually act on — "the team lacks training" is actionable; "humans make mistakes" is not

Common pitfalls

  • Stopping too early at a comfortable, blame-free answer rather than the uncomfortable structural one
  • Following a single causal chain when the problem actually has multiple parallel causes (this is when Fishbone beats 5 Whys)
  • Treating the "5" in 5 Whys literally — sometimes it's 3, sometimes it's 7. Stop when you reach root cause, not at a fixed count
Practice Mode

Apply the framework

Scenario: A community pharmacy chain has seen a sharp rise in prescription dispensing errors over the past 6 months. Run a 5 Whys analysis starting from the presenting problem, and note where you'd want to verify your assumption with real data rather than guessing at the next "why."

Situation Analysis
Gap Analysis
Where you are, where you need to be, and the precise delta in between — the simplest possible diagnostic frame.
Current StateFuture StateDelta

The structure

  • Current state: Where the organisation/process/capability actually is today, evidenced, not assumed
  • Future state: Where it needs to be — tied to a specific goal, strategy, or target
  • The gap: The precise, named difference between the two — this is the actual work to be done
  • Bridging actions: What closes the gap, who owns it, and by when

Why it's distinct from other diagnostic tools

Gap Analysis is the closest consulting comes to science's "knowledge gap" framing — except the gap is usually capability, resource, or outcome, not knowledge. It's less about explaining why a problem exists (that's Root Cause Analysis) and more about precisely defining what needs to change and by how much.

How to run it well

  1. Define future state first and concretely — vague targets produce vague gaps
  2. Assess current state with real evidence (data, audits, interviews) rather than internal assumptions about performance
  3. Name the gap in the same units as the target — if the goal is a number, the gap should be a number too
  4. Resist closing straight to solutions before the gap itself is precisely defined and agreed

Where it shows up across this notebook

  • Scaling Readiness Assessment is a Gap Analysis applied specifically to operational capability
  • OKRs implicitly run a Gap Analysis every cycle — Key Results are the measured gap between current and target state
  • Logic Model and Theory of Change both assume a gap analysis has already identified the outcome worth pursuing
Practice Mode

Apply the framework

Scenario: A regional hospital wants to reduce average emergency department wait times from the current 4.2 hours to a target of 2.5 hours within 18 months. Run a Gap Analysis — define current state, future state, name the precise gap, and propose two bridging actions with owners.

Governance
RACI Matrix
Who is Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, Informed. The antidote to implementation ambiguity.
GovernanceRolesTeam

The four roles

  • Responsible: Does the work. Can be multiple people.
  • Accountable: Owns the outcome. Must be exactly ONE person.
  • Consulted: Provides input. Two-way communication. Consulted before decisions.
  • Informed: Notified after decisions. One-way communication.

Why it matters

Most implementation failures trace to unclear accountability. RACI forces the conversation about who owns what — before the work begins, not after the deadline is missed.

Building one

  1. List all tasks/deliverables down the left column
  2. List all roles/people across the top row
  3. For each task, assign R/A/C/I — ensure exactly one A per row
  4. Review with the team: challenge any row with no A or multiple As
  5. Socialise and lock it before workstreams begin

Red flags to watch for

  • One person is A for everything — overloaded or unclear structure
  • Lots of C and I, few R — decision bottlenecks ahead
  • A role is Consulted for every decision — may actually be a blocker
Practice Mode

Apply the framework

Scenario: You're implementing a new patient data system across 6 hospitals. Roles involved: Project Lead, IT Director, Chief Medical Officer, Frontline Nurses, External Vendor, Hospital Board. Create a partial RACI for these three tasks: (1) System configuration, (2) Staff training design, (3) Go-live decision.

Change Management
Kübler-Ross Change Curve
People don't resist change — they resist loss. Map the emotional journey to plan your interventions.
PeopleAdoptionCulture

The curve stages

  • Shock/Denial: "This won't happen" or "This doesn't affect me"
  • Anger/Fear: "Why is this happening?" or "I'll lose my job"
  • Bargaining: "What if we just did X instead?"
  • Depression: Low energy, disengagement, quiet quitting the change
  • Acceptance: "Okay, let's make this work"
  • Integration: The new becomes the norm

Consulting application

Different stakeholder groups will be at different stages simultaneously. Your change management plan should map interventions to stages — not one-size-fits-all communication.

Interventions by stage

  1. Denial: Provide clear information, share the business case honestly
  2. Anger: Active listening, acknowledge emotions, don't argue
  3. Bargaining: Channel energy into co-design — give people a role
  4. Depression: Quick wins, peer stories, visible progress
  5. Acceptance: Reinforce with training, tools, and early successes

Key insight

  • Leaders typically experience the curve months before their teams — don't assume alignment
  • Unaddressed depression is where implementations die silently
  • Speed through the curve ≠ success — people need time, but they need support, not silence
Practice Mode

Apply the framework

Scenario: A government agency is merging two departments. The announcement was made 6 weeks ago. Frontline workers are disengaged and passive in all-hands meetings. Senior leaders are frustrated with the "lack of buy-in." Diagnose where the workforce likely sits on the curve and recommend three specific interventions.

Project Management
The Consulting Work Plan
Not just a Gantt chart — a living document that connects deliverables, analyses, and owner accountability.
PlanningMilestonesTeam

What makes a consulting work plan different

A consulting work plan ties milestones directly to hypotheses and analyses. Each task should answer: what question does this answer? What deliverable does it feed? Who owns it?

Structure

  • Phase: Discovery / Analysis / Recommendation / Implementation
  • Milestone: The output (e.g. "Client diagnostic complete")
  • Key analyses: The work required to hit that milestone
  • Owner: The single accountable person
  • Due date: Firm, not aspirational
  • Status: On track / At risk / Blocked

How to run it well

  1. Build the work plan with the team, not for the team
  2. Review it at every team meeting — it's a living document
  3. When scope changes, update the work plan explicitly
  4. Flag "at risk" milestones early — surprises destroy trust

The Monday-Friday rhythm

  • Monday: Team sync — what are we proving this week?
  • Wednesday: Mid-week check — any blockers?
  • Friday: Team update — what did we learn, what changes?

Apply the framework

Scenario: You have 8 weeks to deliver a market entry strategy for a healthcare startup entering Southeast Asia. Build the first 2 phases of your work plan: list at least 2 milestones per phase, the 2 key analyses required for each milestone, and the owner role.

Operations Strategy
Value Chain Analysis
Where does your client actually create value — and where do they destroy it? Porter's tool for operational strategy.
PorterOperationsMargin

The two activity types

  • Primary activities (directly create value):
    — Inbound logistics, Operations, Outbound logistics, Marketing & Sales, Service
  • Support activities (enable primary activities):
    — Procurement, Technology development, HR management, Firm infrastructure

Margin = total value created minus total costs of activities. Each activity either adds to margin or erodes it.

The strategic questions

  • Which activities create the most value for the customer?
  • Which activities cost the most relative to value created?
  • Which activities are truly differentiated from competitors?
  • Which activities could be outsourced without losing competitive advantage?
  • Where in the chain can technology create the most leverage?

In consulting practice

  1. Map the client's full value chain — don't assume you know it
  2. For each activity, estimate its cost and the value it adds to the customer
  3. Benchmark against competitors: where are they winning?
  4. Identify linkages — how does one activity affect another?
  5. Focus recommendations on high-cost or high-value activities first

Health sector application

  • In healthcare, primary activities map to: patient intake → diagnosis → treatment → follow-up → discharge
  • Support activities: clinical systems, workforce, procurement, administration
  • The biggest waste is usually in linkages — handoffs between activities where value is lost
Practice Mode

Apply the framework

Scenario: A private hospital group has declining margins despite growing patient volumes. Map their value chain — list at least 3 primary and 2 support activities, identify which activity you'd investigate first as the likely margin problem, and explain which linkage between activities might be the hidden cost driver.

Program Design
Logic Model
Inputs → Activities → Outputs → Outcomes → Impact. The standard architecture for program evaluation.
Theory of ChangeGovernmentSocial Impact

The chain

  • Inputs: Resources invested (money, staff, time, equipment)
  • Activities: What the program does (workshops, services, advocacy)
  • Outputs: Immediate, countable products of activities (# participants, # sessions)
  • Outcomes: Changes in behaviour, knowledge, or conditions
  • Impact: Long-term change in the world attributable to the program

Outputs vs Outcomes: the critical distinction

  • Output: "200 people attended the health literacy workshop" (you control this)
  • Outcome: "Participants increased preventive care visits by 30%" (people changed)
  • Impact: "Reduced emergency admissions across the region" (system changed)

Building one

  1. Start from the right: what impact do you want to achieve?
  2. Work backwards: what outcomes would indicate progress toward that impact?
  3. What activities would drive those outcomes?
  4. What outputs would those activities produce?
  5. What inputs are needed to run those activities?
  6. Validate assumptions at each arrow

Where logic models fail

  • Confusing outputs for outcomes (the most common error)
  • Claiming impact that takes 10 years after a 12-month program
  • Not stating the assumptions that link each step
  • Building it after the program design — should be before
Practice Mode

Apply the framework

Scenario: A nonprofit runs a 6-month mentoring program for young people at risk of unemployment. Build a logic model. For each category (inputs through impact), list 2–3 elements and identify the assumption linking activities to outcomes.

Kirkpatrick Four-Level Model
Did people like it? Did they learn? Did they change? Did it matter? Four levels of training effectiveness.
TrainingL&DROI

The four levels

  • Level 1 – Reaction: Did participants find it relevant and satisfying? (survey)
  • Level 2 – Learning: Did knowledge, skills, or attitudes change? (pre/post test)
  • Level 3 – Behaviour: Are they applying it on the job? (observation, 360)
  • Level 4 – Results: Did organisational outcomes improve? (KPIs, ROI)

The uncomfortable truth

Most training evaluation stops at Level 1 (the happy sheet). Levels 3 and 4 — where real value is — are rarely measured because they require baseline data, time, and a clear line of attribution.

Applying it in consulting

  1. Design the evaluation plan before the training, not after
  2. Establish baselines for Level 3 and 4 metrics before delivery
  3. Build in 90-day and 180-day follow-up checkpoints for behaviour change
  4. Work with the client to agree which Level 4 metrics are attributable

For social impact consulting

  • Level 3 often needs qualitative data — interviews, case studies, observation
  • Level 4 attribution is always contested — use contribution claims, not causation

SWOT Analysis
Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, Threats. Enduring because it forces both internal and external honesty.
StrategyInternalExternal

The four quadrants

  • Strengths: Internal capabilities that give competitive advantage
  • Weaknesses: Internal limitations that put you at a disadvantage
  • Opportunities: External factors you can exploit
  • Threats: External factors that could damage performance

Moving from SWOT to strategy

  • S + O: Use strengths to capture opportunities (aggressive growth)
  • W + O: Address weaknesses to access opportunities (investment)
  • S + T: Use strengths to mitigate threats (defensive)
  • W + T: Minimise weaknesses, avoid threats (risk reduction)

Common SWOT failure modes

  1. It becomes a feel-good exercise — be ruthlessly honest about weaknesses
  2. It ends at the four boxes — always draw out the strategic implications
  3. Internal and external get confused — strengths are internal, opportunities are external
  4. It's done without data — back each point with evidence

Consulting upgrade: TOWS Matrix

After completing the SWOT, build a TOWS Matrix — explicitly cross-matching each S/W/O/T combination to generate strategic options. This turns description into action.

Balanced Scorecard
Four perspectives, one strategy. Kaplan & Norton's system for translating vision into measurable action.
Kaplan NortonStrategy MapKPIs

The four perspectives

  • Financial: How do we look to shareholders/funders? (revenue, cost, profit, ROI)
  • Customer: How do customers see us? (satisfaction, retention, market share, NPS)
  • Internal Processes: What must we excel at? (efficiency, quality, cycle time, innovation rate)
  • Learning & Growth: Can we continue to improve? (staff capability, culture, technology, knowledge)

The causal logic

The BSC is not just four lists of KPIs — it's a causal chain. Learning & Growth enables better Internal Processes, which improves the Customer experience, which drives Financial outcomes. A Strategy Map makes these cause-effect links explicit and visible.

Building one

  1. Start with the Financial perspective: what does success look like in 3 years?
  2. Customer perspective: what must customers experience to drive those financial results?
  3. Internal Processes: what processes must excel to deliver that customer experience?
  4. Learning & Growth: what capabilities, systems, and culture enable those processes?
  5. For each perspective, set 3–5 objectives with KPIs, targets, and initiatives

Public sector & NFP adaptation

  • Replace "Financial" with "Mission Impact" at the top — mission is the ultimate measure
  • Funders and community appear as dual Customer perspectives
  • Financial stewardship becomes a constraint (bottom), not the goal (top)

Economic Evaluation
Cost-Benefit Analysis (CBA)
Do the benefits outweigh the costs — to society, not just the organisation? The standard for government investment decisions.
GovernmentNPVPolicy

CBA vs business case vs SROI

  • Business case: Does this make financial sense for our organisation?
  • SROI: What social value is created per dollar invested?
  • CBA: Do total social benefits (monetised) exceed total social costs? Takes a societal perspective — includes externalities the organisation doesn't bear

The core components

  • Identify all costs: Capital, operating, transition, opportunity costs — over the full project life
  • Identify all benefits: Direct, indirect, and spillover benefits to all affected parties
  • Monetise both: Assign dollar values using market prices, shadow prices, or willingness-to-pay
  • Discount to NPV: Apply discount rate (usually 7% in Australian govt) to make future values comparable
  • BCR: Benefit-Cost Ratio = Total PV Benefits ÷ Total PV Costs. BCR >1 = proceed

In Australian government context

  1. The Australian Government's Investment Framework requires CBA for major projects
  2. Standard discount rate: 7% real (Infrastructure Australia uses 4–10% range)
  3. Sensitivity analysis is mandatory — test BCR under optimistic and pessimistic assumptions
  4. Distributional analysis: who bears the costs and who gets the benefits?

Common traps

  • Double-counting benefits (e.g. counting GDP impact AND productivity gains separately when one causes the other)
  • Choosing a discount rate that makes the project look good
  • Ignoring option value — what's the value of being able to make a different decision later?
  • Treating a BCR of 1.01 as meaningfully different from 0.99 — it isn't, given uncertainty

Ansoff Matrix
Four growth strategies mapped by market and product newness. Essential for scale-up decisions.
GrowthMarketsProducts

The four quadrants

  • Market Penetration: Existing product, existing market — lowest risk, sell more to current customers
  • Market Development: Existing product, new market — moderate risk, find new customers for what you have
  • Product Development: New product, existing market — moderate risk, innovate for known customers
  • Diversification: New product, new market — highest risk, requires new capabilities in unfamiliar territory

How to use it

Map your current strategic options against the matrix. Most organisations instinctively jump to diversification when they should double down on penetration first. The matrix forces an honest conversation about risk tolerance and capability.

For social impact and public sector

  1. Replace "product" with "program/service" and "market" with "community/geography"
  2. Penetration: deepen reach in current communities
  3. Market Development: take a proven program to a new population
  4. Product Development: design a new service for existing beneficiaries
  5. Diversification: new program for a new community — budget and evidence required

Scale-up discipline

  • Most failed scale-ups tried diversification when the penetration strategy wasn't yet exhausted
  • Always sequence: penetrate → develop market → develop product → diversify

Scale-Up Planning
Scaling Readiness Assessment
Are you actually ready to scale? A structured diagnostic across people, process, systems, and capital.
ReadinessCapacityDiagnostic

The four readiness domains

  • People: Do you have the leadership, skills, and culture to scale without breaking?
  • Process: Are your core processes documented, repeatable, and transferable?
  • Systems: Can your technology and data infrastructure handle volume growth?
  • Capital: Do you have the financial runway to absorb scale-up costs before revenue catches up?

The readiness paradox

Many organisations are market-ready before they're internally ready. Scaling demand faster than operational capacity creates service quality failures that damage the very reputation you're trying to build. Pace your external growth to your internal build rate.

Assessment approach

  1. Rate each domain: Ready to Scale / Needs Strengthening / Not Ready
  2. For each "Needs Strengthening" area, identify the specific gap and the time/cost to close it
  3. Identify your single biggest constraint — the bottleneck that limits overall speed
  4. Build your scale-up roadmap around resolving the bottleneck first

Social enterprise addition

  • Add a fifth domain: Evidence: Is your model evidence-based enough to defend at scale?
  • Funders and governments will demand proof before they fund scale

Performance Management
OKRs (Objectives & Key Results)
Ambitious goals linked to measurable evidence. How Google, Intel, and mission-driven organisations align teams to outcomes.
GoalsAlignmentCadence

The structure

  • Objective: Qualitative, inspirational, directional. Answers "where do we want to go?" — memorable, not measurable
  • Key Results: 2–5 per objective. Quantitative, time-bound, specific. Answers "how will we know we got there?"
  • Initiatives: The actual work (projects, tasks) that drive the key results — separate from the OKR itself

Good OKR vs bad OKR

  • Bad KR: "Improve patient satisfaction" — not measurable
  • Good KR: "Increase patient satisfaction NPS from 42 to 60 by Q4" — specific, measurable, time-bound
  • Bad O: "Work on digital transformation" — not inspiring or directional
  • Good O: "Become the most digitally enabled community health service in the state"

Cadence

  1. Annual OKRs set direction
  2. Quarterly OKRs operationalise the plan
  3. Monthly check-ins track progress (1–10 scale or traffic light)
  4. Weekly team standups connect daily work to KRs
  5. End-of-quarter retrospective: what drove the result?

The 70% rule

In OKR culture, consistently scoring 100% means your targets are too easy. Aim to hit 70–80%. If a team consistently scores below 40%, the objective needs to change, not just the effort. This changes the cultural relationship with ambition and failure.

Impact Framework
Theory of Change
If we do X, then Y will happen, because Z. Making your impact assumptions explicit and testable.
Social ImpactNFPGovernment

What it is

A Theory of Change (ToC) is a comprehensive description of how and why a desired change is expected to happen in a particular context. It makes explicit the causal chain from activities to long-term impact — and the assumptions that must hold for each link in the chain.

ToC vs Logic Model

  • Logic Model: Describes what the program does and what it expects to produce
  • Theory of Change: Explains why those activities will produce those outcomes — the mechanism
  • A ToC precedes a logic model. The logic model operationalises the ToC.

Building a ToC

  1. Define the long-term outcome (the change in the world you want)
  2. Identify the preconditions that must be in place for that outcome to occur
  3. Map backwards: what needs to happen first for each precondition?
  4. At each arrow, write the assumption: "This leads to this IF..."
  5. Identify which assumptions are most uncertain — these are your evaluation priorities

The power of making assumptions explicit

  • Most program failures trace to an assumption that was never tested
  • A ToC turns "trust us" into "here's exactly what we believe and why"
  • It gives funders and evaluators a roadmap of where to probe

A note on related treatments in this series

This entry is written from a programme design and evaluation lens — useful for NGOs and government services building or assessing their own programmes. The Impact Investing Playbook has a companion entry, Theory of Change (Investor Lens), that applies the same underlying logic from a capital allocator's seat, with the investor's own contribution made explicit as a distinct, testable link in the chain.

Impact Measurement
Social Return on Investment (SROI)
For every $1 invested, $X of social value was created. Turning social outcomes into a comparable ratio.
FinanceValueEvidence

What it is

SROI is a framework for measuring and communicating the social, environmental, and economic value created by a program or organisation relative to the resources invested. The output is a ratio: "$3.50 of social value for every $1 invested."

The six SROI principles

  • Involve stakeholders
  • Understand what changes
  • Value the things that matter
  • Only include what is material
  • Do not over-claim
  • Be transparent
  • Verify the result

Key adjustments

  1. Deadweight: What would have happened anyway without the program?
  2. Attribution: How much of the change is due to your program vs others?
  3. Displacement: Did improving outcomes here worsen them elsewhere?
  4. Drop-off: Does the impact reduce over time?

Honest limitations

  • SROI ratios are highly sensitive to financial proxies — be transparent about choices
  • High SROI claims (>10:1) are usually overstated — scrutinise assumptions
  • Useful for communication and internal improvement, not as hard evidence

KPI Framework Design
Not all metrics are KPIs. Designing a measurement system that drives the right behaviour — not just reporting.
MetricsDashboardGovernance

KPIs vs metrics vs activities

  • Activity: "Number of meetings held" — you control this, it doesn't tell you if it worked
  • Metric: "Customer satisfaction score" — important but not always critical
  • KPI: A metric that is critical to the achievement of a strategic objective, tracked on a defined cadence with a clear owner and target

Leading vs lagging indicators

  • Lagging: Measure outcomes that have already occurred — can't be changed (revenue, final health outcomes)
  • Leading: Predict future performance — can be influenced now (sales pipeline, early health indicators)
  • A good KPI framework has both — lagging to verify, leading to guide

The SMART test for KPIs

  1. Specific: Clear enough that two people measuring it get the same number
  2. Measurable: Data exists or can be collected feasibly
  3. Achievable: Ambitious but credible
  4. Relevant: Directly linked to a strategic objective
  5. Time-bound: Has a clear review cadence and target date

Goodhart's Law — the fundamental warning

"When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure." Once you measure something, people optimise for it. Design KPIs that are hard to game — and pair them with qualitative sense-checks.

Case Interview
Profitability Framework
Profits = Revenue − Costs. Deceptively simple — the most common case type and the most important to master cold.
MBB CasesRevenueCosts

The full decomposition

  • Revenue: Price × Volume
    — Price: list price, discounts, price mix across segments
    — Volume: # customers × purchase frequency × units per purchase
  • Costs: Fixed + Variable
    — Fixed: rent, salaries, depreciation (don't change with volume)
    — Variable: COGS, commissions, logistics (scale with volume)
  • Profit: Revenue − Total Costs (absolute) or Margin % (relative)

The drill-down discipline

Never just say "revenue declined." Ask: which revenue? All products? One segment? One geography? One channel? The interviewer rewards systematic narrowing — not jumping to solutions before finding the root cause.

Common case structures

  1. Clarify: is profit down because revenue fell, costs rose, or both?
  2. If revenue: is it price or volume? If volume: which product/segment/channel?
  3. If costs: fixed or variable? Which cost line specifically?
  4. Benchmark: is this industry-wide or company-specific?
  5. Synthesise: what's the most likely root cause given all data?

Classic traps

  • Jumping to solutions ("cut costs!") before diagnosing the cause
  • Forgetting to check if it's an industry problem vs company problem
  • Not asking for data — push to quantify every branch
  • Ignoring timing: did anything change when the problem started?

Market Sizing
How big is the market for X in Australia? Structure beats accuracy — show your reasoning, not just a number.
EstimationTAMFermi

Two approaches

  • Top-down: Start with total population → segment → filter → apply penetration rate → multiply by value per customer. Fast but assumption-heavy.
  • Bottom-up: Start with supply-side (# providers × capacity × utilisation × price). Often more defensible.
  • Use both and triangulate — if they agree, you're probably in the right range.

Australian reference numbers to know

  • Population: ~27 million
  • Households: ~10 million (avg 2.5 people)
  • Working age (15–64): ~17 million
  • GDP: ~$2.6 trillion; GDP per capita ~$65K
  • Average household income: ~$110K
  • Healthcare % of GDP: ~10% (~$260B total)

The discipline

  1. Define what you're sizing precisely — TAM, SAM, or SOM?
  2. Choose your approach (top-down or bottom-up) and explain why
  3. Make your assumptions explicit — say them out loud
  4. Do the arithmetic clearly (interviewers watch this)
  5. Sense-check: does the number feel right? Cross-check with a different angle
  6. Round appropriately — never claim false precision

What interviewers reward

  • A clear, logical structure — not the exact right answer
  • Explicit, reasonable assumptions stated confidently
  • Catching your own errors and correcting them
  • A sanity check at the end: "This implies X revenue per provider, which seems plausible because..."

Portfolio Strategy
BCG Growth-Share Matrix
Stars, Cash Cows, Question Marks, Dogs. The original portfolio strategy framework — still the clearest language for resource allocation.
BCGPortfolioInvestment

The four quadrants

  • Stars (high growth, high share): Market leaders in growing markets. Invest heavily — they become tomorrow's Cash Cows.
  • Cash Cows (low growth, high share): Dominant in mature markets. Generate cash; harvest to fund Stars and Question Marks.
  • Question Marks / Problem Children (high growth, low share): Need investment to compete; unclear if they'll become Stars or Dogs. Decide: invest or divest.
  • Dogs (low growth, low share): Weak position in declining market. Divest or restructure unless they serve a strategic purpose.

The cash flow logic

Cash Cows generate excess cash. That cash funds Question Marks that might become Stars. Stars eventually mature into Cash Cows. Dogs drain cash and should be divested. The healthy portfolio has Cash Cows funding the future, not subsidising the past.

How to use it

  1. Define your market clearly — relative market share means share vs your largest competitor
  2. Plot each business unit or product line on the matrix
  3. Assess cash flow generation and requirement for each quadrant
  4. Recommend portfolio rebalancing: invest, hold, harvest, or divest
  5. Show how cash flows between quadrants in your proposed strategy

Limitations to acknowledge

  • Market share ≠ competitive advantage in all industries
  • Dogs can be strategically important (loss-leaders, complements, regulatory requirements)
  • Two dimensions oversimplify complex portfolio decisions
  • The GE-McKinsey 9-box is an upgrade when more nuance is needed
